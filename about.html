Questions & Answers

What is Noumenal Sky?

Noumenal Sky is an educational website that aims to teach users some interesting facts about the objects within our solar system.

How do I use this?

If you come across something you have never heard of and don't understand; jargon such as albedo, or perihelion, simply click on them and a window will appear that will hopefully assist in making clear what those concepts are and what they mean.

What information can I find about each object?

You will find an image of each object with a description of the image. You will find a description of each object, when it was discovered, and where its name comes from. There will be tables with data on the physical and orbital characteristics on each object and more information on technical terms that you don't understand.

I don't understand the terms in the tables

Click on the row on the table that you don't understand to display more information which will explain the meaning of the terms. If you don't find the explanation to be clear enough, please leave us a message and we will fix it.

Where do you get your data?

We source our data from original sources. You can find references for each data point and a link to the original source. For a full list of references click here.

Why study planets?

For this question we will quote the astrophysicist Carl Sagan.
"Exploration is in our nature. We began as wanderers, and we are wanderers still. We have lingered long enough on the shores of the cosmic ocean. We are ready at last to set sail for the stars."

Why is Pluto not a planet?

After the discovery of other Pluto like objects, the International Astronomical Union created a new category of object called a Dwarf Planet. Pluto along with 4 other objects met the definiton of Dwarf Planet rather than planet. This doesn't make Pluto any less special or interesting to study, it's only a categorisation name. Ceres probably got it worse than Pluto, it was orginally a planet but then became an asteroid. Now it is a dwarf planet.

How precise is the data?

Many of the results are rounded. For more precise measurements, you can view the data reference to a particular result. To not overwhelm the user with too much detail, values such as diameter are only approximations. e.g. The Earth is not a perfect sphere. The polar diameter and equatorial diameter are different lengths. We only provide the average diameter.
