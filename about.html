FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

What is Noumenal Sky?

Noumenal Sky gives the user information on objects such as planets, moons, etc in our solar system such as trivia, physical characteristics, and orbital characteristics. We aim to be an educational source, with explanations on what these physical or orbital characteristics are, for users interested in learning more about our solar system. We hope you enjoy your exploration.

How do I use this?

The Home page contains an interactive menu that a user can search through for particular objects they would like to learn more about. If you encounter some jargon you don't understand, you can click on it to be provided with an explanation of that characteristic. Alongside this will be an explantion of the values units and a link to the source of where the value was obtained.

What information can I find about each object?

Each object will have a picture with information on it, such as when, or how it was taken. You will be provided with trivia on the object such as when it was discovered and the origin of its name. You will encounter a table of physical characteristics which include properties such as mass, radius, or bond albedo. You will also encounter a table of orbital characteristics with properties such as, distance, periapsis, or eccentricity. Clicking on a table row will provide you with more information on a particular characteristic.

I don't understand the terms in the tables

If you click on a table row you will be provided with a brief explanation on what the meaning of the term in the table is. If you still don't find it clear enough and are still confused, please contact us and we will try an update it with a better explanation.

Where do you get your data?

We source our data from original sources. You can find references for each data point and a link to the original source. For a full list of references click here.

Why study planets?

For this question we will quote the astrophysicist Carl Sagan.
"Exploration is in our nature. We began as wanderers, and we are wanderers still. We have lingered long enough on the shores of the cosmic ocean. We are ready at last to set sail for the stars."

Why is Pluto not a planet?

After the discovery of other Pluto like objects, the International Astronomical Union created a new category of object called a Dwarf Planet. Pluto along with 4 other objects met the definiton of Dwarf Planet rather than planet. This doesn't make Pluto any less special or interesting to study, it's only a categorisation name.

How precise is the data?

Many of the results are rounded. For more precise measurements, you can view the data reference to a particular result. To not overwhelm the user with too much detail, values such as diameter are only approximations. e.g. The Earth is not a perfect sphere. The polar diameter and equatorial diameter are different lengths. We only provide the average diameter.
